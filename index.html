Cover Investigation

The Forest That Radiation Built: Chernobyl's Extraordinary Wildlife Comeback

By Chris Baraniuk  |  BBC Future  ·  April 26, 2026

Forty years after the explosion at Reactor Four sent clouds of radiation across Europe and forced the evacuation of entire towns, the exclusion zone around Chernobyl has become one of the most unusual ecological experiments on Earth. Scientists visiting the 2,600 km² restricted area — roughly the size of Luxembourg — expected a wasteland. What they found was something far stranger and more wondrous: a landscape teeming with life.

Wolves now roam widely across the zone. Brown bears have reappeared after long absences. European bison move through abandoned farmland. Lynx, elk, moose, and boar occupy habitats once fragmented by agriculture and human settlement. Wolf populations within the exclusion zone are estimated to be seven times higher than in the surrounding nature reserves. The decisive factor, researchers believe, is not radiation tolerance — it is the sudden, total absence of people. Hunting stopped. Roads crumbled. And nature rushed in to fill the silence.

"I expected silence and scarcity — a lifeless place defined by radiation. Instead, I found beavers swimming beneath a nuclear power plant." — Science journalist, first visiting in 2016

Yet the story is not one of simple triumph. Radiation continues to exert real biological pressure, often in subtle and uneven ways. Tree frogs near the plant have been documented with darker skin — possibly an adaptation using elevated melanin as a shield against ionising radiation, according to researcher Pablo Burraco. Birds in higher-radiation areas are more likely to develop cataracts. Critics like Timothy Mousseau caution that correlating such traits with radiation alone is difficult, given the zone's legacy of heavy metal contamination and other stressors. Tidy conclusions, forty years on, continue to elude science.

Megafauna

Przewalski's Horses Roam Free Across Radioactive Steppe

Around 150 of the world's last truly wild horses now range freely through the exclusion zone, introduced in the late 1990s. Sand-coloured and stocky, they graze in landscapes no longer managed by humans — a living symbol of Chernobyl's accidental rewilding.

Adaptation

Darker Frogs, Clouded Eyes: Radiation Leaves Its Quiet Mark

Local tree frogs show unusually dark pigmentation, and birds in the most contaminated areas develop cataracts at elevated rates. Researchers debate whether these are genuine adaptations to radiation or artefacts of other environmental pressures layered over four decades.

New Threats

Russia's Invasion Brings War Into the Exclusion Zone

Russian forces advancing on Kyiv in 2022 dug defensive trenches through contaminated soil and set fire to forests. Drone-triggered blazes now force firefighters to travel dozens of kilometres across minefields, raising fears that radioactive particles are being sent airborne once more.

Ecological Science

The Red Forest's Long Shadow: When Radiation Kills, Then Retreats

Field Report  |  Wild Record Science Desk

In the days after the 1986 explosion, pine forests closest to the plant absorbed such intense radiation that their needles turned a vivid orange-red before the trees died entirely — giving rise to the haunting name "Red Forest." Decades later, those same soils host new growth, though scientists note the recovery is patchy, shaped by contamination and chance rather than any clean ecological narrative.

Conservation Debate

Refuge or Ruin? Scientists Clash Over What Chernobyl's Wildlife Actually Tells Us

Analysis  |  Wild Record

The exclusion zone is celebrated as evidence that nature rebounds when humans retreat, yet researchers remain sharply divided. Some see a flourishing ecosystem; others document invisible harm — shortened lifespans, reproductive stress, subtle mutations — that never makes the headlines. As science writer Mary Mycio argues, Chernobyl is not a return to some pristine past, but the emergence of an entirely new ecosystem shaped by contamination, abandonment, and time.

Long View

Chernobyl as Living Laboratory: What Four Decades of Study Have — and Haven't — Revealed

Feature  |  Wild Record

No other site on Earth offers scientists such a concentrated, long-running window into how ecosystems respond to catastrophic radioactive contamination. Yet the zone's very complexity — radiation, chemical pollution, human absence, and now war — makes extracting clean conclusions nearly impossible. The exclusion zone is likely to remain off-limits for generations, too dangerous for permanent human habitation, yet full of life that defies easy explanation.

Editorial  |  Wild Record

Nature Did Not Forgive Chernobyl. It Simply Moved In.

There is a temptation, in commemorating forty years since the worst nuclear disaster in history, to reach for a story of redemption — wolves where there were none, horses grazing on radioactive steppe, beavers beneath a cooling pond. And that story is true, as far as it goes. But it is not the whole truth. Radiation has not been neutralised by time or by the astonishing resilience of living things. It continues to work at the cellular level, in ways that are quieter than monsters and harder to photograph than a lynx in the snow. What Chernobyl teaches us, four decades on, is not that nature heals all wounds — but that life is stubbornly, improbably persistent even in the face of what we inflict upon it. That ought to inspire humility, not comfort.

— The Editors, Wild Record
